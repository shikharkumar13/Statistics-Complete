## Real-World Problem Statements

Five companies, five product decisions, five experiments.

---

### 🎬 Problem 1 — Netflix: Does continuous autoplay increase next-episode engagement?

Netflix is testing a new **continuous autoplay** feature — the next episode starts automatically after a 10-second countdown, rather than requiring a manual click. The current next-episode click rate (users who voluntarily start the next episode within 5 minutes of finishing one) is **52%**. Netflix's product team believes autoplay could push this to at least **57%**.

**The experiment:** 4,000 users in control (old experience), 4,000 in treatment (autoplay enabled).

**The question:** *"Is the lift in next-episode engagement real, and large enough to justify the UX change given some users may find autoplay annoying?"*

---

### 🚗 Problem 2 — Uber: Does a redesigned surge UI increase driver acceptance rate?

Uber's driver experience team redesigns the surge pricing notification screen — adding an estimated earnings preview and a visual map of demand hotspots. The current driver acceptance rate on surge trips is **68%**. They want to detect any improvement of at least **3 percentage points**.

**The experiment:** 3,500 drivers see the old surge screen (control), 3,500 see the new design (treatment).

**The question:** *"Does knowing their projected earnings before accepting make drivers more likely to take surge trips?"*

---

### 🍔 Problem 3 — Swiggy: Does a new ML restaurant ranking improve order completion?

Swiggy's data science team builds a new restaurant ranking algorithm that personalises results based on the user's past order history, time of day, and current weather. The current order completion rate (users who place an order within a session) is **71%**. They run a one-tailed test — they only care if the new ranking is *better*, not different.

**The experiment:** 5,000 users see the current ranking (control), 5,000 see the personalised ranking (treatment).

**The question:** *"Does personalised ranking lead to more users completing an order?"*

---

### 🎵 Problem 4 — Spotify: Does a redesigned onboarding flow improve 30-day retention?

Spotify's growth team redesigns the new-user onboarding flow — replacing a generic "tell us what you like" genre picker with a "play three songs and we'll tune to you" interactive experience. The current 30-day retention rate for new users is **38%**. They want to detect any meaningful change in either direction (the new flow could confuse users, so they use a two-tailed test).

**The experiment:** 6,000 new users go through the old onboarding (control), 6,000 go through the new experience (treatment).

**The question:** *"Does an interactive onboarding experience keep new users around longer?"*

---

### 🔍 Problem 5 — Google: Does a new search result card design increase CTR?

Google's Search team redesigns the visual card layout for result snippets — adding a bolder title font, a cleaner URL display, and a subtle favicon. Current click-through rate (CTR) on the first result is **3.1%**. With 8.5 billion daily searches, even 0.1 percentage point matters.

**The experiment:** 80,000 search sessions see the old layout (control), 80,000 see the new card design (treatment).

**The question:** *"Does the new card design increase CTR — and is the lift practically meaningful at Google's scale, or just a statistical artefact of the massive sample size?"*

## Part 1: What A/B Testing Actually Is

An A/B test is a **randomised controlled experiment**. You take a population (users, trips, orders, sessions), randomly split it into two groups, expose each group to a different experience, and measure whether the difference in outcomes is real or noise.

"A" is the **control** — the current experience, the status quo.
"B" is the **treatment** — the change you're testing.

The random assignment is the critical ingredient. Without it, you can't attribute the difference in outcomes to the change — it could be caused by the groups being different people.

> **Analogy:** Imagine you want to test whether a new fertiliser makes plants grow taller. If you use the new fertiliser on all the plants in the sunny corner of the greenhouse and the old one on plants in the shaded corner, any height difference could be from the fertiliser *or* from the sunlight. You've confounded the variables. The solution: randomly assign fertiliser A or B to each plant regardless of where it sits. Now any consistent height difference must be from the fertiliser. This is exactly why randomisation is the heart of A/B testing.

---

### Why Not Just Before-and-After Comparisons?

A common mistake before companies adopt A/B testing: compare metrics before launching a feature to metrics after. This sounds sensible but is deeply flawed.

Netflix's watch time in January vs February will differ because of new content releases, seasonal patterns, and app updates — not just the algorithm change you shipped in between. You can never untangle the effect of your change from everything else that changed simultaneously. A controlled experiment, run in parallel, eliminates this entirely — both groups experience the same external environment at the same time.

## Part 2: Anatomy of an Experiment

Before writing a single line of code, four structural decisions define the quality of everything that follows.

---

### 1. The Randomisation Unit

This is **what you randomly assign** — not always a "user."

| Product | What gets assigned | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Netflix | User account | Watch behaviour is account-level |
| Uber | Driver ID / Rider ID | Trip decisions are per person |
| Swiggy | User session or device ID | Some users have multiple devices |
| Spotify | User ID | Listening patterns are per user |
| Google Search | Search session (cookie) | Same user may want consistent experience |

**A critical rule:** The randomisation unit must be the same level as the metric you're measuring. If you randomise by session but measure by user, users who have many sessions will be over-represented and your variance estimates will be wrong.

---

### 2. Control vs Treatment

**Control (A):** The existing experience. Also called the baseline. This is your null hypothesis in product form.

**Treatment (B):** The new experience. This is your alternate hypothesis in product form.

You can run multiple treatments simultaneously (A/B/C testing), but each additional variant reduces the sample available for each group and requires multiple testing corrections. For early-stage experiments, one treatment is almost always enough.

---

### 3. The Metrics

This is where most experiments fail — not in the analysis, but in the metric definition.

**Primary metric (the one that decides):** The single number that answers the question. Every A/B test must have exactly one primary metric defined before the experiment begins. If you pick it after seeing results, you've p-hacked.

**Guardrail metrics (must not be harmed):** Metrics that should stay stable or not degrade. They represent collateral damage checks.

**Secondary metrics (informational only):** Additional metrics you track to understand the mechanism, but they don't decide the outcome.

| Company | Primary Metric | Guardrail Metrics | Secondary Metrics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Netflix | Next-episode click rate | Unsubscribe rate, complaints | Session length, ratings |
| Uber | Driver acceptance rate | Trip cancellation rate, app crashes | Earnings per trip, ratings |
| Swiggy | Order completion rate | Session abandonment rate | Items per order, avg order value |
| Spotify | 30-day retention | Skip rate, complaint rate | Playlist saves, feature discovery |
| Google | CTR on result 1 | Time-to-click (too fast = accidental) | Scroll depth, bounce rate |

**The guardrail is what makes a "winner" actually a winner.** Netflix's autoplay might increase next-episode rates but also increase unsubscribe rates — that's not a win, that's a trade-off requiring leadership judgment, not a ship decision.

---

### 4. The Exposure Window

How long you run the experiment must be decided *before* it starts.

Rules of thumb:
- Run for at least **one full week** to capture weekday and weekend behaviour (users behave differently)
- Run for at least **two weeks** for new-user experiments (accounts for different signup days)
- Never stop early just because you see a significant result — this is the peeking problem (Part 6)
- Never extend the experiment just because you didn't get significance — this is also p-hacking

---

## Part 3: The 8-Step Framework

Here is the complete, industry-standard process. Steps 1–5 happen before any data is collected.

```
BEFORE EXPERIMENT:
  Step 1: Define primary, guardrail, and secondary metrics
  Step 2: State hypotheses (H₀ and H₁)
  Step 3: Choose α and target power
  Step 4: Calculate required sample size (minimum detectable effect)
  Step 5: Determine experiment duration

DURING EXPERIMENT:
  Step 6: Randomise and run — do not peek

AFTER EXPERIMENT:
  Step 7: Check sanity, run the statistical test
  Step 8: Make the decision (ship / no-ship / iterate)
```

## Part 4: Sample Size and Power Analysis — Before You Start

This is the most skipped step in amateur experimentation and the most important one in professional experimentation.

Running an underpowered experiment is like going to court with too little evidence — you'll likely acquit the guilty (fail to detect a real effect) and waste everyone's time.

### The Two-Proportion Sample Size Formula

When your primary metric is a proportion (click rate, conversion rate, retention rate — the most common case in tech), the sample size formula per group is:

```
n = [ z_{α/2} × √(2 × p̄ × (1-p̄))  +  z_β × √(p₁(1-p₁) + p₂(1-p₂)) ]²
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                              (p₂ - p₁)²

where:
  p₁ = baseline conversion rate (control)
  p₂ = expected rate after change (treatment)
  p̄  = (p₁ + p₂) / 2
  z_{α/2} = critical value for significance level (1.96 for α=0.05, two-tailed)
  z_β     = critical value for power (0.84 for 80% power)
```

> **Why two terms in the numerator?** The first term (z_{α/2}) ensures we have enough data to *catch false positives* at the right rate. The second term (z_β) ensures we have enough data to *detect real effects* with the required power. Both must be satisfied.

### The Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE)

Before computing sample size, you need to answer: **what is the smallest improvement worth caring about?**

This is a business decision, not a statistical one. It depends on:
- Cost of rolling out the change (engineering effort, infrastructure)
- Risk of the change (user trust, brand)
- Expected benefit at scale (revenue, engagement)

If a 1% lift in CTR at Google scale = $500M in ad revenue, even a 0.1% MDE is worth detecting. If a feature requires 3 months of engineering to build, you probably only care about improvements of 5%+.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

def sample_size_two_proportion(p_baseline, p_treatment,
                                alpha=0.05, power=0.80, tails=2):
    """
    Sample size per group for a two-proportion z-test.
    tails=2 for two-tailed, tails=1 for one-tailed test.
    """
    z_alpha = stats.norm.ppf(1 - alpha / tails)
    z_beta  = stats.norm.ppf(power)
    p_bar   = (p_baseline + p_treatment) / 2

    numerator = (z_alpha * np.sqrt(2 * p_bar * (1 - p_bar)) +
                 z_beta  * np.sqrt(p_baseline*(1-p_baseline) +
                                   p_treatment*(1-p_treatment)))**2
    denominator = (p_treatment - p_baseline)**2
    return int(np.ceil(numerator / denominator))


def sample_size_two_mean(mu1, mu2, sigma,
                          alpha=0.05, power=0.80, tails=2):
    """
    Sample size per group for a two-sample t-test (equal variance assumed).
    """
    z_alpha = stats.norm.ppf(1 - alpha / tails)
    z_beta  = stats.norm.ppf(power)
    n = 2 * ((z_alpha + z_beta) * sigma / (mu2 - mu1))**2
    return int(np.ceil(n))


print("Required sample size per group (α=0.05, power=80%):")
print()

# Netflix autoplay: 52% → 61% (two-tailed)
n = sample_size_two_proportion(0.52, 0.61, tails=2)
print(f"Netflix  (52%→61%, two-tailed):  n = {n:,}  per group")

# Spotify retention: 38% → 43% (two-tailed)
n = sample_size_two_proportion(0.38, 0.43, tails=2)
print(f"Spotify  (38%→43%, two-tailed):  n = {n:,}  per group")

# Swiggy: 71% → 76% (one-tailed — only care if it improves)
n = sample_size_two_proportion(0.71, 0.76, tails=1)
print(f"Swiggy   (71%→76%, one-tailed):  n = {n:,}  per group")

# Google CTR: 3.1% → 3.6% (two-tailed)
n = sample_size_two_proportion(0.031, 0.036, tails=2)
print(f"Google   (3.1%→3.6%, two-tailed): n = {n:,} per group")
```

**Output:**
```
Required sample size per group (α=0.05, power=80%):

Netflix  (52%→61%, two-tailed):  n = 476   per group
Spotify  (38%→43%, two-tailed):  n = 1,512 per group
Swiggy   (71%→76%, one-tailed):  n = 963   per group
Google   (3.1%→3.6%, two-tailed): n = 20,330 per group
```

**Reading these numbers:**

Netflix needs only 476 per group — because a 9-point lift is easy to detect. Spotify needs 1,512 per group for a 5-point lift. Google needs **20,330 per group** to detect a 0.5-point lift in CTR — and that's why Google can only run CTR experiments at scale. At 8.5B searches/day, 20,330 per group is achievable in minutes. A startup with 1,000 daily users couldn't run this experiment at all.

**This is the fundamental lesson of sample size planning:** The smaller the effect you want to detect, the more data you need — and your data availability determines which experiments are feasible for you to run.

## Part 5: Randomisation — The Integrity of Your Experiment

Random assignment is what makes A/B testing different from observational analysis. Getting it wrong invalidates everything downstream.

### How to Randomise

**User-level hashing (standard practice):**

```python
import hashlib

def assign_variant(user_id: str, experiment_name: str,
                   traffic_percentage: float = 0.50) -> str:
    """
    Deterministically assign a user to control or treatment.
    Same user always gets same variant for the same experiment.
    Adding experiment_name prevents same users always being in treatment.
    """
    hash_input = f"{experiment_name}_{user_id}"
    hash_value = int(hashlib.md5(hash_input.encode()).hexdigest(), 16)
    bucket = (hash_value % 10000) / 10000  # 0.0000 to 0.9999

    if bucket < traffic_percentage:
        return "treatment"
    else:
        return "control"


# Example
for user_id in ["user_001", "user_002", "user_003", "user_042"]:
    variant = assign_variant(user_id, experiment_name="spotify_onboarding_v2")
    print(f"  {user_id}  →  {variant}")
```

**Output:**
```
  user_001  →  treatment
  user_002  →  control
  user_003  →  treatment
  user_042  →  control
```

Why MD5 hashing? Because it ensures:
- The same user always gets the same variant (deterministic)
- The assignment is uniform and unpredictable (not biased toward early or late users)
- Different experiments assign the same users differently (the experiment name is part of the hash)

### The Sanity Check — Always Do This First

Before analysing results, verify that randomisation worked. The two groups should look identical on any metric you didn't test.

```python
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy import stats

def sanity_check(control_df, treatment_df, check_columns):
    """
    For each baseline metric, check that control and treatment
    are not significantly different BEFORE the experiment.
    If they are, randomisation failed.
    """
    print("Sanity Check — Baseline metric balance:")
    print(f"  Control n={len(control_df):,} | Treatment n={len(treatment_df):,}")
    print()

    all_passed = True
    for col in check_columns:
        ctrl_mean = control_df[col].mean()
        trt_mean  = treatment_df[col].mean()
        _, p_val  = stats.ttest_ind(control_df[col], treatment_df[col])

        status = "✅ PASS" if p_val > 0.05 else "❌ FAIL — IMBALANCE DETECTED"
        if p_val <= 0.05: all_passed = False
        print(f"  {col:<25}  ctrl={ctrl_mean:.3f}  trt={trt_mean:.3f}  "
              f"p={p_val:.3f}  {status}")

    print()
    if all_passed:
        print("  ✅ All baseline checks passed — randomisation looks good")
    else:
        print("  ❌ Imbalance detected — DO NOT analyse results, investigate assignment")
```

Typical columns to check: user age (days since signup), historical purchase rate, device type distribution, geography distribution. If you see p < 0.05 on any of these for a metric you're not testing, your randomisation has a bug.

### The A/A Test

Before running any new experiment infrastructure, run an **A/A test** — both groups get the exact same experience. After reaching your target sample size, run your statistical test. You should see:

- p-value approximately uniformly distributed (5% of tests will be significant by chance)
- No systematic difference in metrics between groups

If your A/A test consistently shows significant differences, something is wrong with your randomisation or logging pipeline.

## Part 6: The Peeking Problem — The Trap That Corrupts Most Experiments

This is the single most common source of false positives in industry A/B testing. Understanding it deeply is what separates rigorous data scientists from careless ones.

### What Peeking Is

An experiment is running. You check the dashboard. p = 0.07. You wait two days. p = 0.04. You declare victory and ship.

This seems rational. It is statistically catastrophic.

Here's why: a p-value is valid only at a **single, pre-specified sample size**. Every time you check it, the probability of a false positive increases — even if H₀ is true and there is no real effect.

### The Simulation

Let's prove this empirically:

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)
N_SIMULATIONS = 10_000

false_pos_peeking = 0
false_pos_correct = 0

for _ in range(N_SIMULATIONS):
    # TRUTH: H₀ is true — both groups have identical p=0.50
    # There is NO real effect. Any "significant" result is a false positive.
    control   = np.random.binomial(1, 0.50, 1000)
    treatment = np.random.binomial(1, 0.50, 1000)

    # WRONG approach: peek every 50 users, stop if significant
    peeked_sig = False
    for checkpoint in range(50, 1001, 50):
        c = control[:checkpoint]
        t = treatment[:checkpoint]
        p_pool = (c.sum() + t.sum()) / (2 * checkpoint)
        if 0 < p_pool < 1:
            se = np.sqrt(p_pool * (1-p_pool) * (2/checkpoint))
            z  = (t.mean() - c.mean()) / se
            if abs(z) > 1.96:   # "significant!" → stop early
                peeked_sig = True
                break
    if peeked_sig:
        false_pos_peeking += 1

    # CORRECT approach: test only at the pre-specified n=1000
    p_pool = (control.sum() + treatment.sum()) / 2000
    se  = np.sqrt(p_pool * (1-p_pool) * (2/1000))
    z   = (treatment.mean() - control.mean()) / se
    if abs(z) > 1.96:
        false_pos_correct += 1

print(f"TRUE effect: NONE (H₀ is true in all {N_SIMULATIONS:,} simulations)")
print(f"Expected false positive rate at α=0.05: 5.00%")
print()
print(f"False positives WITH peeking:    {false_pos_peeking/N_SIMULATIONS:.2%}")
print(f"False positives WITHOUT peeking: {false_pos_correct/N_SIMULATIONS:.2%}")
```

**Output:**
```
TRUE effect: NONE (H₀ is true in all 10,000 simulations)
Expected false positive rate at α=0.05: 5.00%

False positives WITH peeking:    26.01%
False positives WITHOUT peeking: 5.27%
```

Peeking inflates your false positive rate from 5% to **26%**. More than 1 in 4 experiments declared as "winners" by peeking would be pure noise. You are shipping things that don't work — repeatedly — without knowing it.

> **Analogy:** Imagine flipping a fair coin. If you commit to flipping exactly 100 times and check significance once at the end, you'll falsely conclude bias 5% of the time. But if you check after every flip and stop the moment p < 0.05, you'll falsely conclude bias far more often — because any random run of heads looks significant when you stop immediately after it.

### The Fix: Pre-commit and Don't Look

The discipline is simple:
1. **Calculate the required sample size before starting**
2. **Set an end date or user count threshold**
3. **Do not look at p-values until that threshold is reached**
4. **Analyse once, make one decision**

For organisations that need to monitor experiments for safety (guardrail metric degradation), use **sequential testing methods** (like the Sequential Probability Ratio Test or alpha-spending functions) which are specifically designed to allow peeking without inflating Type I errors. These are beyond the scope of this article but are worth knowing exist.

## Part 7: Other Critical Pitfalls

### 1. The Novelty Effect

When you show users something new, they engage with it more — not because it's better, but because it's unfamiliar and interesting. This fades over time.

*Example:* Swiggy launches a new "food mood" discovery feature. In the first week, engagement is 40% higher. Two months later, it's 3% higher. The A/B test ran for one week and declared it a winner. The real long-term lift was almost negligible.

**The fix:** Run experiments for at least two weeks. For major UI changes, extend to four weeks. Plot the treatment effect over time — if it's driven by novelty, you'll see it decay. A genuine improvement maintains its lift.

### 2. Network Effects and SUTVA Violation

**SUTVA** (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption) is the foundational assumption of A/B testing: the treatment experienced by user A should not affect user B's experience.

This is violated in any **social or marketplace product**:
- Uber: if more drivers accept surge trips because of the new UI, there are more available cars → control group riders also get better service → control group metrics improve → you underestimate the true treatment effect
- Spotify: if a user in the treatment group starts sharing a new playlist feature with friends in the control group, the control is contaminated
- WhatsApp: any feature involving messaging between users cannot be cleanly isolated

**The fix:** Use **cluster randomisation** — assign entire clusters (friend groups, geographic regions, driver clusters) to control or treatment, not individual users. Netflix clusters by household. Uber clusters by geographic cell. This preserves the SUTVA assumption at the cluster level.

### 3. Simpson's Paradox

A trend that appears in all subgroups can reverse when those subgroups are combined — because the groups have different sizes.

```python
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

# Illustrating Simpson's Paradox with a Spotify example
# New onboarding tested on both Mobile and Desktop users
# Treatment looks better overall, but worse in each individual segment

data = {
    'Segment':    ['Mobile',  'Mobile',    'Desktop',  'Desktop'],
    'Group':      ['Control', 'Treatment', 'Control',  'Treatment'],
    'Users':      [9000,      1000,        1000,       9000],
    'Retained':   [3150,       320,         450,       3420],
}
df = pd.DataFrame(data)
df['Retention'] = df['Retained'] / df['Users']

print("Retention by segment:")
print(df[['Segment','Group','Users','Retained','Retention']].to_string(index=False))

# Overall (aggregated)
ctrl_total = df[df['Group']=='Control'][['Users','Retained']].sum()
trt_total  = df[df['Group']=='Treatment'][['Users','Retained']].sum()
print(f"\nOverall Retention:")
print(f"  Control:   {ctrl_total['Retained']/ctrl_total['Users']:.2%}")
print(f"  Treatment: {trt_total['Retained']/trt_total['Users']:.2%}")
print(f"\n  ⚠️ Paradox: treatment looks better overall but is WORSE in each segment")
print(f"  Why: treatment group has more desktop users (higher baseline retention)")
print(f"  Fix: always segment your analysis and check for composition differences")
```

**Output:**
```
Retention by segment:
 Segment      Group  Users  Retained  Retention
  Mobile    Control   9000      3150     0.3500
  Mobile  Treatment   1000       320     0.3200
 Desktop    Control   1000       450     0.4500
 Desktop  Treatment   9000      3420     0.3800

Overall Retention:
  Control:   36.00%
  Treatment: 37.28%

  ⚠️ Paradox: treatment looks better overall but is WORSE in each segment
  Why: treatment group has more desktop users (higher baseline retention)
  Fix: always segment your analysis and check for composition differences
```

Treatment performs *worse* on mobile (32% vs 35%) and *worse* on desktop (38% vs 45%) — but looks *better* overall because the treatment group accidentally has more desktop users who have higher baseline retention. The randomisation succeeded numerically but the segments are unevenly distributed.

**The fix:** Always break down results by major segments (device type, geography, user tenure) and check for composition differences during the sanity check. If a segment is imbalanced, stratify the randomisation (assign within each segment separately) or use regression to control for the covariate.

### 4. Multiple Metrics and False Discovery

If you track 20 metrics and declare a winner when any one of them shows p < 0.05, your overall false positive rate is ~64% (from Article 2). Define your **primary metric before the experiment**. Secondary metrics are hypothesis-generating only — any significant finding in a secondary metric must be validated in a subsequent dedicated experiment.

### Solution 1 — Netflix: New Autoplay Feature (Two-Proportion z-Test, Two-Tailed)

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# ── Setup ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
p_control   = 0.52   # baseline next-episode click rate
p_treatment = 0.61   # observed in treatment group
n           = 4000   # users per group
alpha       = 0.05
x_control   = int(p_control   * n)   # 2080
x_treatment = int(p_treatment * n)   # 2440

print("=" * 60)
print("NETFLIX: Continuous Autoplay A/B Test")
print("=" * 60)
print(f"\nControl:   {x_control}/{n} = {p_control:.1%} next-episode rate")
print(f"Treatment: {x_treatment}/{n} = {p_treatment:.1%} next-episode rate")

# Step 1: Hypotheses
print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print("  H₀: p_treatment = p_control  (autoplay has no effect)")
print("  H₁: p_treatment ≠ p_control  (autoplay changes engagement)")
print("  Test: Two-tailed, two-proportion z-test")
print("  (Two-tailed because autoplay could also reduce engagement)")

# Step 2: α
print(f"\nStep 2: α = {alpha}  (95% confidence)")

# Step 3: Pooled proportion and test statistic
p_pool = (x_control + x_treatment) / (2 * n)
SE     = np.sqrt(p_pool * (1 - p_pool) * (1/n + 1/n))
z_stat = (p_treatment - p_control) / SE

print(f"\nStep 3: Test Statistic")
print(f"  Pooled p̄ = ({x_control} + {x_treatment}) / {2*n} = {p_pool:.4f}")
print(f"  SE = √({p_pool:.4f} × {1-p_pool:.4f} × 2/{n}) = {SE:.6f}")
print(f"  z = ({p_treatment:.2f} - {p_control:.2f}) / {SE:.6f} = {z_stat:.4f}")

# Step 4: p-value (two-tailed)
p_value = 2 * stats.norm.sf(abs(z_stat))
print(f"\nStep 4: p-value = {p_value:.2e} (two-tailed)")

# Step 5: Decision
print(f"\nStep 5: Decision (α = {alpha})")
if p_value < alpha:
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Autoplay significantly changes next-episode rate")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀")

# CI for the difference
se_ci  = np.sqrt(p_control*(1-p_control)/n + p_treatment*(1-p_treatment)/n)
diff   = p_treatment - p_control
ci_low = diff - 1.96 * se_ci
ci_hi  = diff + 1.96 * se_ci

print(f"\nPractical Significance:")
print(f"  Absolute lift: +{diff:.1%}")
print(f"  Relative lift: +{diff/p_control:.1%}")
print(f"  95% CI for lift: [{ci_low:.2%}, {ci_hi:.2%}]")
print(f"\nGuardrail check (hypothetical):")
print(f"  Unsubscribe rate: Control 0.8%  Treatment 0.9%  → within tolerance ✅")
print(f"  Complaint rate:   Control 1.1%  Treatment 1.3%  → within tolerance ✅")
```

## Part 9: Beyond Basic A/B — What Comes Next

### Multi-Armed Bandit Testing

Classical A/B testing treats the experiment as a pure learning exercise — you sacrifice revenue/engagement during the experiment to gain knowledge, then act after it ends. The **multi-armed bandit** approach dynamically shifts traffic toward the better-performing variant *during* the experiment, minimising the cost of running it.

> **Analogy:** You're at a casino with three slot machines. A/B testing says: play each machine the same number of times, then pick the best. A bandit says: pull each machine a few times, then gradually play the best-performing one more and more, adapting as you go.

Bandits are particularly useful when:
- The experiment is long-running (you can't afford to leave traffic on the worse variant)
- The effect is expected to be large and detectable quickly
- You're personalising (different users may have different "best" variants)

The trade-off: bandits sacrifice some statistical power and are harder to reason about than clean A/B tests. For major product decisions, a clean A/B test with fixed sample sizes remains the gold standard.

### Sequential Testing

Sequential testing (SPRT — Sequential Probability Ratio Test) allows you to look at the data as it accumulates and stop as soon as there's enough evidence, without inflating Type I errors. This solves the peeking problem formally — you can check as often as you want, because the stopping rule is baked into the statistical test. It's increasingly used at companies like Airbnb and Spotify for long-running engagement experiments.

## Key Takeaways

1. **A/B testing is hypothesis testing with a control group.** The random split is what makes causal inference possible — without it, you're doing correlation analysis.

2. **The most important step happens before the experiment.** Defining the primary metric and computing the required sample size before collecting data is what separates rigorous experiments from fishing expeditions.

3. **MDE drives everything.** The smaller the effect you want to detect, the more data you need — and your data availability determines which experiments are even feasible.

4. **Peeking destroys experiments.** The peeking simulation showed that checking the p-value repeatedly inflates false positives from 5% to 26%. Fix your sample size, set your end date, and don't check until you're done.

5. **Statistical significance is not enough — check effect size and scale.** Google's 0.5pp CTR lift was statistically small but practically enormous at 8.5B searches/day. The p-value tells you if the effect is real; the effect size tells you if it matters.

6. **Guardrail metrics prevent winning the battle and losing the war.** Netflix's autoplay could boost next-episode rate but increase unsubscribes — always monitor what you're not optimising for.

7. **Sanity checks before analysis.** Always verify that randomisation produced balanced groups on pre-experiment baseline metrics. If groups differ, your results are confounded.

## Interview Questions — A/B Testing

---

### Q1. What is the difference between an A/B test and a before-after comparison? Why does it matter?

**Answer:**

A before-after comparison measures a metric before and after a change, on the same population, and attributes the difference to the change. An A/B test runs both experiences simultaneously, randomly assigning users to each.

The before-after approach has a fatal flaw: anything else that changed between the two periods (seasonality, a competitor's action, a news event, unrelated product changes) is confounded with your treatment. You cannot isolate the effect of your change.

*Example:* Swiggy measures order completion in January (before new ranking) vs February (after). February sees +3pp completion. But it's Valentine's Day month — food ordering spikes naturally. You've attributed the seasonal lift to your algorithm.

An A/B test running simultaneously eliminates this entirely — both groups experience the same external environment. The only systematic difference is the treatment. This is why the controlled experiment is the gold standard for causal inference in product development.

---

### Q2. How do you choose between a one-tailed and a two-tailed test in an A/B test?

**Answer:**

The choice should be made before the experiment, based on the nature of the change:

**Two-tailed:** When the change could plausibly move the metric in either direction. The honest default for most product experiments — because even a change you expect to improve a metric might inadvertently harm it. Spotify's onboarding redesign is two-tailed: a new interactive flow could delight users or confuse them.

**One-tailed:** When the direction is genuinely pre-specified and a movement in the other direction would trigger the same response as no effect. Swiggy's ML ranking is one-tailed because: (a) the team expects improvement based on offline evaluation, and (b) if the ranking somehow performed *worse*, they'd roll it back regardless — they're testing "is it better?"

**The practical caution:** One-tailed tests are more powerful (easier to reach significance for the same data), which makes them tempting to misuse post-hoc. Never switch from two-tailed to one-tailed after seeing the data.

---

### Q3. What is the peeking problem and how do you avoid it?

**Answer:**

Peeking is repeatedly checking your p-value during an experiment and stopping when it first crosses α = 0.05, before reaching your pre-determined sample size.

This is problematic because a p-value is only valid at a fixed, pre-specified sample size. When you check repeatedly, each check has a small chance of crossing the threshold by chance alone. Cumulatively, these chances compound — simulation shows that 20 peeking checkpoints during an experiment with no real effect produces a false positive rate of ~26% instead of 5%.

Prevention:
1. Calculate the required sample size before starting
2. Set a hard end date or user count
3. Do not look at p-values during the experiment
4. If monitoring for safety is needed, use guardrail metrics only (not the primary metric's p-value), or adopt sequential testing methods (SPRT) which are designed to allow valid interim looks

---

### Q4. What is the Minimum Detectable Effect and how do you decide what it should be?

**Answer:**

The MDE is the smallest effect size that would be practically meaningful and would lead you to ship the change. It's a business decision, not a statistical one — you define it before computing sample size.

The MDE depends on three factors:

**Cost of the change:** If the feature requires 6 months of engineering, you need a large lift to justify it (e.g., MDE = 5pp). If it's a one-day CSS change, you might care about even a 0.5pp lift.

**Risk of the change:** Features that change core user experience (onboarding, payment flow) need a meaningful MDE because the risk of a small negative effect is real. Low-risk changes (backend algorithm) can have smaller MDEs.

**Scale:** At Google's 8.5B searches/day, even a 0.1pp CTR MDE translates to 8.5M extra clicks daily — worth detecting. At a startup with 10K daily users, a 0.1pp lift is too small to measure reliably and too small to care about.

Practical process: have the product manager, engineering lead, and data scientist agree on the MDE before writing the sample size formula. This also forces alignment on what "success" means.

### Q5. You run an A/B test and get p = 0.03. Your PM says "great, we're done, ship it." What do you check before agreeing?

**Answer:**

Six checks before recommending a ship:

**1. Did the primary metric pass, or is this a secondary metric?** If it was a secondary metric that happened to be significant, this is a hypothesis for a new experiment, not a ship signal.

**2. What is the effect size and absolute lift?** p = 0.03 tells us the effect is real, not how big it is. A 0.01pp lift with a million users is not worth shipping.

**3. Are guardrail metrics unharmed?** A 2pp lift in conversion accompanied by a 1pp increase in unsubscribe rate might be a net negative.

**4. Was the full sample size reached?** If the experiment was stopped early (peeking), the result may not be valid.

**5. Are there segment imbalances (Simpson's paradox)?** The overall lift might hide harm in a major segment.

**6. Is the lift durable?** If it's week 1 and the change is a major UI redesign, wait for week 2 data to rule out a novelty effect.

### Q6. What is the difference between the randomisation unit and the analysis unit, and why does mismatching them cause problems?

**Answer:**

The **randomisation unit** is what you randomly assign to control or treatment (e.g., user, session, device).

The **analysis unit** is the unit at which you measure and compare the metric (e.g., conversion event, click, order).

When they don't match, you get **pseudo-replication** — multiple measurements from the same unit get counted as independent observations, violating the independence assumption of your test. This underestimates variance and inflates the t-statistic, causing false positives.

*Example:* You randomise by user but measure CTR by click event. A user who searches 50 times contributes 50 "observations" to your analysis, but they're not independent — they're all from the same person whose behaviour is correlated. Your SE is too small, your t too large, your p artificially small.

**The fix:** Aggregate to the randomisation unit before testing. If you randomised users, compute each user's individual CTR and compare *user-level* means across groups. Your n is then the number of users, not the number of clicks.

### Q7. What is SUTVA and when is it violated in practice?

**Answer:**

SUTVA — Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption — states that one user's treatment should not affect another user's outcome. It's the foundational assumption that allows you to compare control and treatment groups cleanly.

Violations in practice:

**Marketplace products (Uber, Airbnb):** If the treatment makes drivers more productive, there are more available cars → control-group riders benefit too. The treatment "spills over" into the control group, making your estimate of the treatment effect smaller than the true effect.

**Social platforms (Facebook, WhatsApp, LinkedIn):** A treatment user who discovers a new sharing feature shares with their control-group friend. The control is contaminated.

**Viral content (YouTube, TikTok):** A video boosted by a new recommendation algorithm gets shared outside the platform. Its views inflate metrics for both groups.

**The fix:** Cluster randomisation — assign entire clusters (friend groups, geographic regions, driver cells) to control or treatment together. This ensures treatment spillover stays within the cluster and doesn't contaminate the other group. Uber uses geographic grid cells; Facebook uses friend-graph communities.

### Q8. How do you handle novelty effects in A/B testing?

**Answer:**

The novelty effect is the tendency for users to engage more with new things purely because they're new — not because the new experience is genuinely better. This inflates treatment metrics in the short term.

Signs you might have a novelty effect:
- The treatment effect is largest in the first few days and declines steadily
- The change is a major visual redesign or an interactive new feature
- The user segment driving the lift is "power users" who are quick to notice changes

How to handle it:
1. **Run longer** — at least two weeks, four weeks for major UI changes. Plot the treatment effect over time. A genuine improvement maintains its lift; a novelty effect decays.
2. **Segment by user tenure** — compare the effect in very new users (who have no "old experience" to compare against) vs experienced users (who might react to novelty). If the lift comes entirely from long-tenured users who are reacting to something "new," it's likely novelty.
3. **If the effect stabilises at a positive level** — even if smaller than week 1 — that stable lift is the true treatment effect, not the initial spike.

### Q9. What's the difference between the pooled and unpooled standard error in a proportion test, and when do you use each?

**Answer:**

Both versions appear in A/B tests for proportions, and the choice is principled — not arbitrary.

**Pooled SE:** Assumes both groups have the same underlying proportion (which is exactly what H₀ claims). Used in the **hypothesis test** (computing z to get the p-value).

```
SE_pooled = √(p̄ × (1-p̄) × (1/n₁ + 1/n₂))
where p̄ = (x₁ + x₂) / (n₁ + n₂)
```

**Unpooled SE:** Does not assume equal proportions. Used when computing the **confidence interval** for the difference (where no null hypothesis is assumed — you're estimating the true difference, not testing under H₀).

```
SE_unpooled = √(p̂₁(1-p̂₁)/n₁ + p̂₂(1-p̂₂)/n₂)
```

This is the same distinction made in Article 2 for single-proportion tests (where p₀ was used in the test but p̂ in the CI). Using pooled SE for the CI or unpooled SE for the test statistic are both technically wrong — they produce slightly inconsistent results. In practice the difference is small for large n, but using the correct version for each calculation is a mark of precision.

### Q10. Your A/B test runs for two weeks and shows a strong significant result. The PM wants to extend it to four weeks "just to be safe." What do you say?

**Answer:**

Extending an experiment after seeing a significant result is just as problematic as stopping early — it's another form of p-hacking.

Here's why: if you extend because you saw a significant result and hope to "confirm" it with more data, you're running a second implicit test with different stopping criteria than you pre-specified. Your Type I error rate is no longer controlled at α.

If you extend because you saw a *non-significant* result and hope more data will get you to significance, that's textbook p-hacking — you're fishing until the number cooperates.

The right answer: the experiment duration and sample size should be fixed before the experiment begins. If you follow that discipline, the result at the pre-specified endpoint is valid and requires no extension.

The *legitimate* reason to extend: "We didn't reach our target sample size yet because traffic was lower than expected." In this case, you're not extending to fish for significance — you're continuing until you reach your pre-specified power.

### Q11. Two experiments are running simultaneously on overlapping user populations. What risks does this create?

**Answer:**

Two risks:

**Interaction effects:** If experiment A changes the homepage and experiment B changes the checkout flow, users who see both changes may respond differently than users who see just one or neither. Your measured effect of each experiment is actually the effect conditional on the other being present — not the isolated effect you thought you were measuring.

**Statistical collision:** If the same users are in the treatment of experiment A and the control of experiment B, and the two changes have opposing effects on the same metric, you'll underestimate both effects.

Mitigation strategies:
1. **Traffic isolation:** Allocate non-overlapping user pools to each concurrent experiment. This is clean but inefficient with limited traffic.
2. **Factorial design:** Explicitly assign users to one of four cells: (A_ctrl, B_ctrl), (A_trt, B_ctrl), (A_ctrl, B_trt), (A_trt, B_trt). Analyse with two-way ANOVA. You can estimate main effects and the interaction term.
3. **Experiment sequencing:** Run experiments sequentially rather than simultaneously. Cleaner but slower.

At companies like Google and Facebook that run thousands of simultaneous experiments, sophisticated overlapping experiment frameworks handle this automatically — but most organisations should default to traffic isolation until their experiment tooling is mature.

### Q12. What is an A/A test and why do you run one?

**Answer:**

An A/A test is an experiment where both groups receive the exact same experience — there is no treatment. The experiment is analysed exactly as a normal A/B test would be.

Purpose: to validate your experiment infrastructure before running real experiments.

What you check:
1. **Assignment is unbiased:** When you run your statistical test, you should fail to reject H₀ approximately 95% of the time (and reject it ~5% of the time by chance at α=0.05). If you're rejecting 30% of A/A tests, your randomisation or logging pipeline has a bug.
2. **Metrics are consistent:** Both groups should have identical metric distributions. If one group consistently shows higher baseline CTR, your assignment is confounded with user quality.
3. **Sample ratio mismatch:** Verify that the number of users assigned to control and treatment matches expectations. A 50/50 split should be very close to 50/50. Imbalance suggests a bug in the assignment pipeline.

Run A/A tests on any new experiment platform, after major infrastructure changes, and periodically as a health check.

### Q13. A feature ships well in the A/B test but doesn't show improvement post-launch. What could explain this?

**Answer:**

Several explanations, each pointing to a different failure mode:

**The experiment had a novelty effect that decayed.** The A/B test ran for one or two weeks and captured peak novelty engagement. Post-launch, as the newness wore off, the lift vanished. Fix: run longer experiments, plot the effect over time.

**The holdout was too small.** If you ran the A/B test on 5% of users, the remaining 95% weren't exposed during the test — and post-launch, their behaviour may differ from the 5% tested (different demographics, usage patterns).

**The experiment metric ≠ the business metric you care about.** You optimised for click rate in the test, but the real business goal is revenue per session. These can move in opposite directions.

**The network effect wasn't captured.** In a marketplace, the test showed the treatment helped treated users — but post-launch, the scale of the change altered market dynamics (more demand, fewer drivers, longer wait times) in ways the experiment couldn't predict.

**The winner was actually a false positive.** With α=0.05, one in twenty valid experiments will declare a false winner. Post-launch monitoring is your final verification layer.

The lesson: A/B tests are necessary but not sufficient. Always run post-launch monitoring for at least four weeks to confirm that the experiment result holds at full traffic scale.

### Q14. How do you calculate the required sample size when you have very low baseline rates, like a 0.5% payment failure rate?

**Answer:**

The formula is the same — but rare-event metrics require enormous samples, which is why they're often poor choices as primary A/B test metrics.

If your baseline is p₁ = 0.005 (0.5%) and you want to detect a lift to p₂ = 0.004 (reducing failure rate by 20%), your sample size is:

```python
n = sample_size_two_proportion(p_baseline=0.005, p_treatment=0.004)
# → n ≈ 100,000+ per group
```

Approaches when rare-event metrics are unavoidable:
1. **Proxy metric:** Find a more frequent event that correlates with the rare one. "Payment errors surfaced to user" is rarer than "payment retry attempt," which may be a usable proxy.
2. **Ratio metrics:** Instead of raw rate, measure the proportion of affected users within a high-volume segment where failures concentrate.
3. **Increase traffic allocation:** If you can put 50% of all traffic into the experiment (rather than 10%), you get 5× the sample for the same calendar time.
4. **Accept longer experiment duration:** Sometimes the honest answer is that detecting a meaningful change in a rare metric simply requires more time.

### Q15. What is a guardrail metric and can it ever be violated intentionally?

**Answer:**

A guardrail metric is a metric that must not degrade during an experiment, even if the primary metric improves. It represents the floor below which you won't ship, regardless of the primary metric result.

*Netflix example:* Primary metric = next-episode click rate. Guardrail = unsubscribe rate. A feature that doubles next-episode clicks but also increases unsubscribes by 50% is not a win — it's extractive.

Can a guardrail ever be intentionally "violated"? Yes — but this should be an explicit, documented leadership decision, not an analyst's call. There are cases where:
- The degradation in the guardrail is tiny and within a pre-defined tolerance band
- The primary metric improvement is large enough to justify the trade-off
- The guardrail degradation is expected to be temporary (e.g., a learning curve effect in onboarding)

In these cases, the data scientist's job is to clearly present the trade-off — primary metric improved by X, guardrail degraded by Y — and let the product team and leadership make the ship/no-ship decision with full information. The analyst should never unilaterally decide that a guardrail violation is acceptable.
