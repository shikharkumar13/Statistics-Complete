# Inferential Statistics for Data Scientists
## Article 2: Hypothesis Testing — Making Evidence-Based Decisions

> **Continuing from Article 1:** You learned how to estimate population values using samples — confidence intervals, CLT, Bootstrap. But estimation only answers "what is the value?" Hypothesis testing answers a harder question: **"Is what we're seeing real, or just noise?"** This is the engine behind every A/B test, product experiment, and data-driven decision at every major tech company.

---

## The Bridge from Article 1

In Article 1, Spotify sampled 10,000 users and built a 95% CI for their conversion rate: [11.36%, 12.64%]. That told us where the true rate probably lives.

Now suppose Spotify's growth team redesigned the Premium upgrade page and ran it for a sample of users. They see a new conversion rate of 13.5%. The question is no longer "where is the rate?" — it's:

**"Is 13.5% genuinely better than our baseline of 12%, or did we just get lucky with this sample?"**

That is a hypothesis test. And answering it rigorously — without fooling yourself — is one of the most important skills in data science.

## Real-World Problem Statements from Big Tech

Here are five problems from the same companies in Article 1, now framed as hypothesis testing questions.

---

### 🎬 Problem 1 — Netflix: Did the new recommendation algorithm increase watch time?

Netflix's engineering team ships a new recommendation engine. The old algorithm produced an average watch time of **108 minutes/day**. They run the new algorithm on a sample of **1,200 users** and observe a mean of **112 minutes/day** with a standard deviation of **47 minutes**.

**The question:** *"Is the improvement of 4 minutes real, or could it be random sampling variation? Should we roll out the new algorithm to all 260M users?"*

---

### 🚗 Problem 2 — Uber: Did route optimisation reduce trip durations?

Uber's maps team rolls out a new routing algorithm in Mumbai. Historical data shows the average trip duration was **22 minutes**. A sample of **800 trips** under the new algorithm shows a mean of **20.3 minutes** with a standard deviation of **11 minutes**.

**The question:** *"Is 1.7 minutes of improvement statistically significant, or could we see this just by chance in any random sample of 800 trips?"*

---

### 🍔 Problem 3 — Swiggy: Does the new partner incentive bring delivery time under 30 minutes?

Swiggy's operations team introduces a new delivery partner incentive scheme. They sample **400 deliveries** under the new scheme. Sample mean: **28.4 minutes**, standard deviation: **6.8 minutes**.

**The question:** *"Can we formally claim the true mean delivery time is under 30 minutes, or is this sample result insufficient evidence?"*

---

### 🎵 Problem 4 — Spotify: Did the redesigned Premium page improve conversion?

Spotify redesigns their Premium upgrade page. In the old version, conversion rate was **12%**. They show the new page to **8,000 free users** and observe **1,080 conversions** (13.5%).

**The question:** *"Is 13.5% significantly higher than 12%? Did the redesign actually work, or is this a fluke?"*

---

### 📺 Problem 5 — YouTube: Is watch time different between mobile and desktop users?

YouTube's product team wants to know if mobile and desktop users consume content differently. They sample **600 mobile users** (mean: 42 min/day, std: 18 min) and **500 desktop users** (mean: 38 min/day, std: 22 min).

**The question:** *"Is the 4-minute difference between mobile and desktop users real, or within the range of random noise? Should the product team build separate content strategies for each platform?"*

---

Each of these is a different flavour of the same core question: **Is the pattern we observed in the sample strong enough to believe it's true in the population?** Let's build the framework to answer that rigorously.

## Part 1: The Big Idea — Innocent Until Proven Guilty

Hypothesis testing borrows its logic directly from the justice system.

In a court, the defendant is presumed **innocent until proven guilty**. The prosecution must present evidence strong enough to overturn that presumption. The jury doesn't ask "is the defendant innocent?" — they ask "does the evidence against them cross the threshold for conviction?"

Hypothesis testing works the same way:
- The **null hypothesis (H₀)** is the presumption of innocence — the conservative claim that nothing changed, nothing worked, there is no effect.
- The **alternate hypothesis (H₁)** is the prosecution's claim — the new thing worked, the difference is real, the effect exists.
- The **data (sample)** is the evidence.
- The **significance threshold (α)** is the standard of proof.
- The **p-value** tells you how strong the evidence is against the null.

You never "prove" H₁ is true. You only assess whether the evidence is strong enough to **reject H₀**. Just like a jury doesn't declare someone innocent — they declare "not guilty" (insufficient evidence to convict).

> **Analogy:** You're a quality inspector at Swiggy. The default assumption is that delivery times are fine (H₀: mean ≥ 30 minutes, i.e., no improvement). Your new incentive scheme is on trial. You collect 400 deliveries and ask: if the incentive scheme did nothing, how likely is it we'd see a sample mean this low just by chance? If that probability is very small, the evidence is compelling — reject H₀ and conclude the scheme works.

## Part 2: The Five-Step Framework

Every hypothesis test — regardless of type — follows the same five steps. Learn this framework and you can apply it to any test you'll ever encounter.

```
Step 1: State the hypotheses (H₀ and H₁)
Step 2: Choose the significance level (α)
Step 3: Compute the test statistic
Step 4: Find the p-value
Step 5: Make a decision — reject H₀ or fail to reject H₀
```

Let's break each step down.

---

### Step 1: State the Hypotheses

**Null Hypothesis (H₀):** The default, conservative claim. Always contains an equality (=, ≤, or ≥). Always what you're trying to disprove.

**Alternate Hypothesis (H₁):** The claim you're trying to support with evidence. What you believe is true if H₀ is wrong.

Examples:

| Business Question | H₀ | H₁ |
|---|---|---|
| Netflix algorithm improved watch time? | μ_new ≤ 108 min | μ_new > 108 min |
| Swiggy delivery time under 30 min? | μ ≥ 30 min | μ < 30 min |
| Spotify redesign improved conversion? | p_new ≤ 0.12 | p_new > 0.12 |
| YouTube: mobile ≠ desktop watch time? | μ_mobile = μ_desktop | μ_mobile ≠ μ_desktop |

**One-tailed vs Two-tailed tests:**

- **One-tailed test:** H₁ specifies a direction — "greater than" or "less than." Use when you only care about improvement (or only about decline). Netflix, Uber, Swiggy, Spotify above are all one-tailed.
- **Two-tailed test:** H₁ says "different from" — no direction specified. Use when a difference in either direction matters. YouTube above is two-tailed.

> **Analogy:** One-tailed is like asking "Did the new medicine make patients better?" Two-tailed is like asking "Did the new medicine change patient outcomes at all?" The second question catches both improvements and unexpected harm.

---

### Step 2: Choose the Significance Level (α)

The **significance level (α)** is the probability of making a wrong rejection — concluding the effect is real when it isn't. It's the threshold that defines "strong enough evidence."

Common choices:

| α | Equivalent Confidence | Typical Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| 0.10 | 90% | Exploratory analysis, low-stakes decisions |
| **0.05** | **95%** | **Standard in most data science and research** |
| 0.01 | 99% | High-stakes decisions (medical, financial, legal) |

Choose α *before* looking at your data. Changing α after seeing the results is called **p-hacking** — one of the most common and dangerous forms of statistical malpractice.

> **Analogy:** α is like setting the conviction threshold *before* the trial begins. A threshold of α = 0.05 means: "We'll only convict if there's less than a 5% chance this evidence would appear if the defendant were truly innocent." Changing the threshold after seeing the evidence is rigging the trial.

---

### Step 3: Compute the Test Statistic

The test statistic converts your sample result into a standardised score — it tells you how many standard errors away from the null hypothesis your observation is.

**For means (z-statistic, large n):**
```
z = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s / √n)
```

**For means (t-statistic, small n or unknown σ):**
```
t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s / √n)     with df = n - 1
```

*(Same formula — the difference is which distribution table you use to find the p-value.)*

**For two-sample means:**
```
t = (x̄₁ - x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
```

**For proportions:**
```
z = (p̂ - p₀) / √(p₀(1 - p₀) / n)
```

> **Analogy:** The test statistic is like converting height into a z-score. Instead of saying "this person is 185 cm," you say "this person is 1.8 standard deviations above average." It puts every result on the same universal scale so we can compare across problems.

---

### Step 4: Find the p-value

The **p-value** is the probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as (or more extreme than) the one you got, *assuming H₀ is true*.

A small p-value means: "If H₀ were true, seeing this result would be very unlikely — so maybe H₀ isn't true."

**What the p-value is NOT:**
- It is NOT the probability that H₀ is true
- It is NOT the probability that you made an error
- It is NOT the probability that your result was due to chance

**What it IS:**
- The probability of your data (or something more extreme) given H₀ is true
- A measure of how surprising your evidence is, assuming the null holds

> **Analogy:** You're flipping a coin you suspect is biased toward heads. H₀ is that it's fair. You flip 20 times and get 17 heads. The p-value asks: "If this coin were perfectly fair, what's the probability of getting 17 or more heads in 20 flips?" If that probability is tiny (say, 0.001), the data is very surprising under H₀ — compelling evidence the coin is biased.

---

### Step 5: Make the Decision

```
If p-value ≤ α  →  Reject H₀  →  "Statistically significant"
If p-value > α  →  Fail to reject H₀  →  "Not statistically significant"
```

**Crucial language distinction:**
- "Fail to reject H₀" does NOT mean "H₀ is true." It means "we don't have enough evidence to reject it."
- "Reject H₀" does NOT mean "H₁ is definitely true." It means "the data is inconsistent with H₀ at significance level α."

---

## Part 3: Type I and Type II Errors — The Two Ways to Be Wrong

Every decision in a hypothesis test can be wrong in one of two ways. Understanding these errors is critical — they have very different consequences, and different industries tolerate them differently.

```
                     Reality
                  H₀ True    H₀ False
Decision  Reject  TYPE I ❌   Correct ✅
          Fail    Correct ✅  TYPE II ❌
         to Reject
```

### Type I Error (False Positive) — α

- **What it is:** Rejecting H₀ when it's actually true. Concluding the new algorithm works when it doesn't.
- **Probability:** α (the significance level you chose)
- **Controlled by:** Lowering α reduces Type I errors

*Netflix example:* Concluding the new recommendation engine increased watch time and rolling it out to 260M users — when in reality the sample difference was just luck, and the algorithm is no better than the old one.

### Type II Error (False Negative) — β

- **What it is:** Failing to reject H₀ when it's actually false. Missing a real effect.
- **Probability:** β (not directly controlled by α)
- **Controlled by:** Increasing sample size, increasing effect size, raising α

*Netflix example:* The new algorithm genuinely does improve watch time, but your sample was too small to detect it, so you fail to reject H₀ and shelve the algorithm — losing the improvement.

### Statistical Power = 1 - β

**Power** is the probability of correctly detecting a real effect when it exists. It's what you *want* to be high.

```
Power = 1 - β
```

A test with 80% power (β = 0.20) will detect a true effect 80% of the time, and miss it 20% of the time. 80% power is the conventional minimum in most industries.

**What increases power:**
- Larger sample size (the biggest lever)
- Larger true effect size (you can't control this, but knowing it helps plan)
- Higher α (but this also increases Type I errors — a trade-off)
- Lower data variability (collect cleaner data, reduce noise)

> **Analogy:** Type I error is a fire alarm going off when there's no fire. Type II error is a fire burning while the alarm stays silent. α controls false alarms. Power controls how reliably the alarm goes off in real fires. Reducing false alarms (lower α) tends to make the alarm less sensitive (lower power) — you can't eliminate both without more data.

## Part 4: The Tests — Which One to Use and When

Different data situations require different test statistics. Here's the complete decision map:

```
What are you testing?
│
├── One sample vs a known value
│   ├── Comparing a MEAN to μ₀
│   │   ├── σ known (rare)        →  One-sample z-test
│   │   └── σ unknown (almost always) →  One-sample t-test
│   │       (for large n, t ≈ z — the choice barely matters)
│   └── Comparing a PROPORTION to p₀  →  One-sample z-test for proportions
│
└── Two samples vs each other
    ├── Comparing two MEANS (independent groups)  →  Two-sample t-test (Welch's)
    ├── Comparing two MEANS (same group, before/after)  →  Paired t-test
    └── Comparing two PROPORTIONS  →  Two-proportion z-test
```

### One-Sample z/t Test
Use when: comparing your sample mean to a known benchmark (historical value, industry standard, SLA target).

### Two-Sample t-Test (Welch's)
Use when: comparing means from two independent groups (control vs treatment, mobile vs desktop). Welch's version doesn't assume equal variances — always prefer it over the classical pooled t-test.

### Paired t-Test
Use when: the same subjects are measured twice (before/after). The pairs remove individual-level variability, making the test more sensitive.

### Proportion z-Test
Use when: your outcome is binary (converted/didn't convert, churned/retained) and you're comparing a rate to a baseline or between two groups.

## Part 5: Statistical Significance vs Practical Significance

This is the most under-taught concept in hypothesis testing, and arguably the most important one for data scientists working in industry.

**Statistical significance** tells you: "Is the effect real, or could it be noise?"

**Practical significance** tells you: "Is the effect large enough to matter?"

These are completely independent. A result can be:
- **Statistically significant but practically useless** — At large n, even a difference of 0.001 minutes in watch time will have p < 0.05. But does 0.001 minutes matter to Netflix? Absolutely not.
- **Practically meaningful but not statistically significant** — A small sample might miss a real 10-minute improvement in delivery time simply because n is insufficient.

### Effect Size — Quantifying Practical Significance

The most common measure for means is **Cohen's d**:

```
Cohen's d = (x̄₁ - x̄₂) / s_pooled
```

Interpretation:
| d | Practical Significance |
|---|---|
| 0.2 | Small effect |
| 0.5 | Medium effect |
| 0.8 | Large effect |

For proportions, the raw difference (p̂₁ - p̂₂) is often the most intuitive measure. A lift from 12% to 12.1% has p < 0.05 with a million users — but it's not worth shipping a redesign for.

> **Analogy:** Imagine a drug trial shows the new medication reduces blood pressure by 0.1 mmHg with p = 0.0001. Statistically significant (very large sample). But a 0.1 mmHg change is clinically meaningless — it's smaller than the error in a blood pressure cuff. Statistical significance without practical significance is a false victory.

**The rule in industry:** Always report both the p-value AND the effect size (or the raw difference). Make the business decision based on both, not just p < 0.05.

## Part 6: The Multiple Testing Problem

Suppose you're at YouTube testing 20 different changes to the app simultaneously. You use α = 0.05 for each test. What's the probability of getting at least one false positive across all 20 tests?

```
P(at least one false positive) = 1 - (1 - 0.05)²⁰ = 1 - 0.95²⁰ ≈ 0.64
```

There's a 64% chance you'd declare at least one "winner" that's actually noise. This is the **multiple testing problem** — a major source of false discoveries in industry.

### The Fix: Bonferroni Correction

Divide your significance threshold by the number of tests:

```
α_adjusted = α / number of tests
```

If you're running 20 tests and want an overall α = 0.05:
```
α_adjusted = 0.05 / 20 = 0.0025
```

Now each individual test uses a stricter threshold, keeping the overall false positive rate at or below 5%.

The Bonferroni correction is conservative (it sometimes misses real effects). More powerful alternatives exist (Benjamini-Hochberg, FDR control), but Bonferroni is the intuitive starting point and is sufficient for most interview discussions.

```python
# Quick illustration
import numpy as np

n_tests = 20
alpha = 0.05

prob_at_least_one_false_positive = 1 - (1 - alpha) ** n_tests
print(f"Without correction: P(≥1 false positive) = {prob_at_least_one_false_positive:.2%}")

alpha_bonferroni = alpha / n_tests
print(f"Bonferroni threshold per test: α = {alpha_bonferroni:.4f}")

prob_after_correction = 1 - (1 - alpha_bonferroni) ** n_tests
print(f"After Bonferroni: P(≥1 false positive) = {prob_after_correction:.2%}")
```

**Output:**
```
Without correction: P(≥1 false positive) = 64.15%
Bonferroni threshold per test: α = 0.0025
After Bonferroni: P(≥1 false positive) = 4.88%
```

### Solution 1 — Netflix: One-Sample t-Test (Did the new algorithm improve watch time?)

**Setup:** Old algorithm baseline μ₀ = 108 min/day. New algorithm tested on n = 1,200 users: x̄ = 112 min, s = 47 min. Did it improve?

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Given
mu_0 = 108        # baseline mean (minutes)
x_bar = 112       # sample mean under new algorithm
s = 47            # sample standard deviation
n = 1200          # sample size
alpha = 0.05      # significance level

print("=" * 55)
print("NETFLIX: Did the new algorithm increase watch time?")
print("=" * 55)

# Step 1: Hypotheses
print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print(f"  H₀: μ ≤ {mu_0} min  (no improvement)")
print(f"  H₁: μ >  {mu_0} min  (improvement exists)")
print(f"  Test type: One-tailed (right-tailed)")

# Step 2: Significance level
print(f"\nStep 2: Significance level α = {alpha}")

# Step 3: Test statistic
SE = s / np.sqrt(n)
t_stat = (x_bar - mu_0) / SE

print(f"\nStep 3: Test Statistic")
print(f"  Standard Error = {s} / √{n} = {SE:.3f}")
print(f"  t = ({x_bar} - {mu_0}) / {SE:.3f} = {t_stat:.3f}")

# Step 4: p-value (one-tailed, right tail)
p_value = stats.t.sf(t_stat, df=n-1)  # sf = 1 - cdf (right tail)

print(f"\nStep 4: p-value")
print(f"  p-value = {p_value:.6f}")

# Step 5: Decision
print(f"\nStep 5: Decision")
if p_value <= alpha:
    print(f"  p-value ({p_value:.6f}) ≤ α ({alpha})")
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Statistically significant improvement")
else:
    print(f"  p-value ({p_value:.6f}) > α ({alpha})")
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀ — Insufficient evidence")

# Effect size
cohens_d = (x_bar - mu_0) / s
print(f"\nEffect Size (Cohen's d) = {cohens_d:.3f}")
print(f"  Interpretation: {'Small' if cohens_d < 0.2 else 'Medium' if cohens_d < 0.5 else 'Large'} effect")
```

**Output:**
```
=======================================================
NETFLIX: Did the new algorithm increase watch time?
=======================================================

Step 1: Hypotheses
  H₀: μ ≤ 108 min  (no improvement)
  H₁: μ >  108 min  (improvement exists)
  Test type: One-tailed (right-tailed)

Step 2: Significance level α = 0.05

Step 3: Test Statistic
  Standard Error = 47 / √1200 = 1.357
  t = (112 - 108) / 1.357 = 2.948

Step 4: p-value
  p-value = 0.001630

Step 5: Decision
  p-value (0.001630) ≤ α (0.05)
  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Statistically significant improvement

Effect Size (Cohen's d) = 0.085
  Interpretation: Small effect
```

**Business Interpretation:**

The test strongly rejects H₀ (p = 0.0016 << 0.05). The 4-minute improvement in watch time is statistically real — it is not a sampling fluke. However, Cohen's d = 0.085 indicates a **small effect size**. Netflix's leadership should ask: is a 4-minute increase (3.7% lift) worth the infrastructure cost of the new algorithm? Statistical significance says the effect is real. Business judgment must decide if it's worth it.

### Solution 2 — Uber: One-Sample t-Test (Did route optimisation reduce trip duration?)

**Setup:** Baseline μ₀ = 22 min. New algorithm on n = 800 trips: x̄ = 20.3 min, s = 11 min. Did it reduce trip time?

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

mu_0 = 22
x_bar = 20.3
s = 11
n = 800
alpha = 0.05

print("=" * 55)
print("UBER: Did route optimisation reduce trip duration?")
print("=" * 55)

# Step 1
print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print(f"  H₀: μ ≥ {mu_0} min  (no reduction)")
print(f"  H₁: μ < {mu_0} min  (duration reduced)")
print(f"  Test type: One-tailed (left-tailed)")

# Step 3
SE = s / np.sqrt(n)
t_stat = (x_bar - mu_0) / SE

print(f"\nStep 3: Test Statistic")
print(f"  SE = {SE:.4f},   t = {t_stat:.4f}")

# Step 4: left-tailed p-value
p_value = stats.t.cdf(t_stat, df=n-1)

print(f"\nStep 4: p-value = {p_value:.8f}")

# Step 5
print(f"\nStep 5: Decision (α = {alpha})")
if p_value <= alpha:
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Significant reduction in trip duration")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀")

cohens_d = abs(x_bar - mu_0) / s
print(f"\nEffect Size (Cohen's d) = {cohens_d:.3f} — "
      f"{'Small' if cohens_d < 0.2 else 'Medium' if cohens_d < 0.5 else 'Large'} effect")

# What does 1.7 minutes mean at scale?
daily_trips_mumbai = 500_000
minutes_saved_per_day = (mu_0 - x_bar) * daily_trips_mumbai
print(f"\nScale impact in Mumbai:")
print(f"  1.7 min × 500,000 trips/day = {minutes_saved_per_day:,.0f} minutes saved/day")
print(f"  = {minutes_saved_per_day/60:,.0f} driver-hours saved per day")
```

**Output:**
```
=======================================================
UBER: Did route optimisation reduce trip duration?
=======================================================

Step 1: Hypotheses
  H₀: μ ≥ 22 min  (no reduction)
  H₁: μ < 22 min  (duration reduced)
  Test type: One-tailed (left-tailed)

Step 3: Test Statistic
  SE = 0.3889,   t = -4.3710

Step 4: p-value = 0.00000694

Step 5: Decision (α = 0.05)
  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Significant reduction in trip duration

Effect Size (Cohen's d) = 0.155 — Small effect

Scale impact in Mumbai:
  1.7 min × 500,000 trips/day = 850,000 minutes saved/day
  = 14,167 driver-hours saved per day
```

**Business Interpretation:**

The p-value is vanishingly small (0.0000069). The 1.7-minute reduction is absolutely real — not noise. Despite a small Cohen's d (0.155), the **scale of the business transforms this into massive practical significance**: 14,000+ driver-hours saved every single day in one city. This is a textbook example of why effect size alone doesn't tell the full story in industry. At Uber's scale, even small per-trip improvements compound enormously.

### Solution 3 — Swiggy: One-Sample t-Test (Is mean delivery time truly under 30 minutes?)

**Setup:** Swiggy claims ≤ 30 minutes. New incentive scheme on n = 400 deliveries: x̄ = 28.4 min, s = 6.8 min. Can we formally support this claim?

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

mu_0 = 30         # SLA threshold
x_bar = 28.4
s = 6.8
n = 400
alpha = 0.05

print("=" * 55)
print("SWIGGY: Is true mean delivery time under 30 min?")
print("=" * 55)

print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print(f"  H₀: μ ≥ {mu_0} min  (SLA is NOT being met)")
print(f"  H₁: μ < {mu_0} min  (SLA IS being met)")
print(f"  Test type: One-tailed (left-tailed)")

SE = s / np.sqrt(n)
t_stat = (x_bar - mu_0) / SE
p_value = stats.t.cdf(t_stat, df=n-1)

print(f"\nStep 3: SE = {SE:.4f},   t = {t_stat:.4f}")
print(f"Step 4: p-value = {p_value:.8f}")

print(f"\nStep 5: Decision (α = {alpha})")
if p_value <= alpha:
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Strong evidence mean delivery < 30 min")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀ — Cannot confirm SLA is met")

# 95% CI for context
t_crit = stats.t.ppf(0.975, df=n-1)
MoE = t_crit * SE
print(f"\n95% Confidence Interval: [{x_bar - MoE:.2f}, {x_bar + MoE:.2f}] minutes")
print(f"The entire CI is below 30 min: {(x_bar + MoE) < 30}")
```

**Output:**
```
=======================================================
SWIGGY: Is true mean delivery time under 30 min?
=======================================================

Step 1: Hypotheses
  H₀: μ ≥ 30 min  (SLA is NOT being met)
  H₁: μ < 30 min  (SLA IS being met)
  Test type: One-tailed (left-tailed)

Step 3: SE = 0.3400,   t = -4.7059
Step 4: p-value = 0.00000162

Step 5: Decision (α = 0.05)
  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Strong evidence mean delivery < 30 min

95% Confidence Interval: [27.73, 29.07] minutes
The entire CI is below 30 min: True
```

**Business Interpretation:**

Hypothesis testing and the CI from Article 1 tell the same consistent story — they always do. The p-value is tiny and the entire 95% CI [27.7, 29.1 min] sits comfortably below 30 minutes. Swiggy's ops team can confidently announce to leadership that the new incentive scheme is working, with formal statistical backing. Notice also how the CI gives *more* information (where the true mean is), while the hypothesis test gives a binary verdict (is it below 30?). In practice, report both.

### Solution 4 — Spotify: One-Sample Proportion z-Test (Did the redesign improve conversion?)

**Setup:** Baseline conversion p₀ = 12%. New page shown to n = 8,000 users: 1,080 conversions (p̂ = 13.5%). Was the redesign effective?

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

p_0 = 0.12        # baseline proportion
x = 1080          # observed conversions
n = 8000          # sample size
p_hat = x / n     # sample proportion
alpha = 0.05

print("=" * 55)
print("SPOTIFY: Did the redesigned page improve conversion?")
print("=" * 55)

print(f"\nObserved p̂ = {x}/{n} = {p_hat:.4f} ({p_hat*100:.1f}%)")

print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print(f"  H₀: p ≤ {p_0}  (redesign did NOT improve conversion)")
print(f"  H₁: p >  {p_0}  (redesign improved conversion)")
print(f"  Test type: One-tailed (right-tailed), proportion z-test")

# Step 3: Proportion z-statistic
# Note: use p_0 (not p_hat) in the SE formula for hypothesis testing
SE = np.sqrt(p_0 * (1 - p_0) / n)
z_stat = (p_hat - p_0) / SE

print(f"\nStep 3: Test Statistic")
print(f"  SE = √({p_0} × {1-p_0} / {n}) = {SE:.5f}")
print(f"  z = ({p_hat:.4f} - {p_0}) / {SE:.5f} = {z_stat:.4f}")

# Step 4: p-value (right-tailed)
p_value = stats.norm.sf(z_stat)

print(f"\nStep 4: p-value = {p_value:.8f}")

print(f"\nStep 5: Decision (α = {alpha})")
if p_value <= alpha:
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Redesign significantly improved conversion")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀")

# Practical significance
lift = p_hat - p_0
print(f"\nPractical Significance:")
print(f"  Absolute lift = {lift:.4f} = {lift*100:.1f} percentage points")
print(f"  Relative lift = {lift/p_0*100:.1f}%")
print(f"\n  Projected impact on 350M free users:")
print(f"  Extra conversions = {lift * 350_000_000:,.0f}")
```

**Output:**
```
=======================================================
SPOTIFY: Did the redesigned page improve conversion?
=======================================================

Observed p̂ = 1080/8000 = 0.1350 (13.5%)

Step 1: Hypotheses
  H₀: p ≤ 0.12  (redesign did NOT improve conversion)
  H₁: p >  0.12  (redesign improved conversion)
  Test type: One-tailed (right-tailed), proportion z-test

Step 3: Test Statistic
  SE = √(0.12 × 0.88 / 8000) = 0.00363
  z = (0.1350 - 0.12) / 0.00363 = 4.1300

Step 4: p-value = 0.00001815

Step 5: Decision (α = 0.05)
  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Redesign significantly improved conversion

Practical Significance:
  Absolute lift = 0.0150 = 1.5 percentage points
  Relative lift = 12.5%

  Projected impact on 350M free users:
  Extra conversions = 5,250,000
```

**Business Interpretation:**

Both statistically and practically significant. The redesign produces a 12.5% relative lift in conversion (12% → 13.5%), and the p-value of 0.000018 is far below any reasonable α. At Spotify's scale, 1.5 percentage points translates to **5.25 million additional Premium subscribers** — potentially hundreds of millions of dollars in annual recurring revenue. This is a clear ship decision.

**One important note on the SE formula:** In hypothesis testing for proportions, the standard error uses p₀ (the null hypothesis value), not p̂ (the sample proportion). This is because under H₀, we assume p = p₀ is true — that's the world we're computing probability under. This is subtly different from the CI formula in Article 1, which correctly uses p̂ (since no null hypothesis is assumed there).

### Solution 5 — YouTube: Two-Sample t-Test (Is watch time different across devices?)

**Setup:** Mobile users (n₁ = 600, x̄₁ = 42 min, s₁ = 18 min) vs Desktop users (n₂ = 500, x̄₂ = 38 min, s₂ = 22 min). Is the 4-minute difference real?

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Mobile
n1, x_bar1, s1 = 600, 42, 18

# Desktop
n2, x_bar2, s2 = 500, 38, 22

alpha = 0.05

print("=" * 60)
print("YOUTUBE: Is watch time different between mobile & desktop?")
print("=" * 60)

print("\nStep 1: Hypotheses")
print(f"  H₀: μ_mobile = μ_desktop  (no difference)")
print(f"  H₁: μ_mobile ≠ μ_desktop  (difference exists)")
print(f"  Test type: Two-tailed, two-sample Welch's t-test")

# Step 3: Welch's t-statistic (does not assume equal variances)
SE = np.sqrt(s1**2 / n1 + s2**2 / n2)
t_stat = (x_bar1 - x_bar2) / SE

# Welch-Satterthwaite degrees of freedom
df_num = (s1**2/n1 + s2**2/n2)**2
df_den = (s1**2/n1)**2 / (n1 - 1) + (s2**2/n2)**2 / (n2 - 1)
df = df_num / df_den

print(f"\nStep 3: Test Statistic (Welch's t)")
print(f"  SE = √({s1}²/{n1} + {s2}²/{n2}) = {SE:.4f}")
print(f"  t = ({x_bar1} - {x_bar2}) / {SE:.4f} = {t_stat:.4f}")
print(f"  Welch-Satterthwaite df = {df:.1f}")

# Step 4: Two-tailed p-value
p_value = 2 * stats.t.sf(abs(t_stat), df=df)

print(f"\nStep 4: p-value = {p_value:.6f}")
print(f"  (Multiplied by 2 for two-tailed test)")

print(f"\nStep 5: Decision (α = {alpha})")
if p_value <= alpha:
    print(f"  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Significant difference between platforms")
else:
    print(f"  ❌ FAIL TO REJECT H₀ — No significant difference detected")

# Effect size
s_pooled = np.sqrt(((n1-1)*s1**2 + (n2-1)*s2**2) / (n1 + n2 - 2))
cohens_d = abs(x_bar1 - x_bar2) / s_pooled
print(f"\nEffect Size (Cohen's d) = {cohens_d:.3f}")
print(f"  Interpretation: {'Small' if cohens_d < 0.2 else 'Medium' if cohens_d < 0.5 else 'Large'} effect")

# 95% CI for the difference
t_crit = stats.t.ppf(0.975, df=df)
MoE = t_crit * SE
diff = x_bar1 - x_bar2
print(f"\n95% CI for (μ_mobile - μ_desktop): [{diff - MoE:.2f}, {diff + MoE:.2f}] minutes")
```

**Output:**
```
============================================================
YOUTUBE: Is watch time different between mobile & desktop?
============================================================

Step 1: Hypotheses
  H₀: μ_mobile = μ_desktop  (no difference)
  H₁: μ_mobile ≠ μ_desktop  (difference exists)
  Test type: Two-tailed, two-sample Welch's t-test

Step 3: Test Statistic (Welch's t)
  SE = √(18²/600 + 22²/500) = √(0.540 + 0.968) = 1.2280
  t = (42 - 38) / 1.2280 = 3.2573
  Welch-Satterthwaite df = 961.7

Step 4: p-value = 0.001164
  (Multiplied by 2 for two-tailed test)

Step 5: Decision (α = 0.05)
  ✅ REJECT H₀ — Significant difference between platforms

Effect Size (Cohen's d) = 0.201 — Medium effect

95% CI for (μ_mobile - μ_desktop): [1.59, 6.41] minutes
```

**Business Interpretation:**

The 4-minute difference is real (p = 0.001164). Mobile users genuinely watch more per day than desktop users. The 95% CI [1.59, 6.41] minutes tells us the true difference is likely between 1.6 and 6.4 minutes — mobile could be anywhere from modestly to substantially more engaged. Cohen's d = 0.201 sits right at the Small-to-Medium boundary, and at YouTube's scale even the lower end of that CI (a 1.6-minute difference per user per day) represents billions of cumulative minutes globally. YouTube's product team should confidently pursue separate content and recommendation strategies per platform — backed by this evidence.

## Part 8: The Complete Decision Cheatsheet

```
HYPOTHESIS TESTING — QUICK REFERENCE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

SETUP
  H₀: the boring claim (no effect, no change, nothing happened)
  H₁: what you're trying to prove (effect exists, improvement real)
  α: set BEFORE seeing data (usually 0.05)

CHOOSE YOUR TEST
  One sample vs known value
    → mean:       one-sample t-test    t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n)
    → proportion: proportion z-test    z = (p̂ - p₀) / √(p₀(1-p₀)/n)

  Two independent samples
    → means:      Welch's two-sample t-test
    → proportions: two-proportion z-test

  Same group measured twice
    → paired t-test  (compute differences, run one-sample t-test)

TAIL DIRECTION
  "greater than"  → right-tailed  →  p = P(T > t_stat)
  "less than"     → left-tailed   →  p = P(T < t_stat)
  "different from"→ two-tailed    →  p = 2 × P(T > |t_stat|)

DECISION
  p ≤ α  →  Reject H₀  →  "Statistically significant"
  p > α  →  Fail to reject H₀  →  "Not enough evidence"

ALWAYS ALSO REPORT
  → Effect size (Cohen's d, or raw lift for proportions)
  → Confidence interval for the effect
  → Business interpretation (not just p < 0.05)

WATCH OUT FOR
  → Multiple testing → Bonferroni correction
  → Large n making tiny effects "significant"
  → Changing α after seeing results (p-hacking)
  → Confusing "fail to reject H₀" with "H₀ is true"

```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hypothesis testing is structured decision-making.** The five-step framework (state hypotheses, choose α, compute test statistic, find p-value, decide) applies to every test you'll ever run.

2. **H₀ is always the conservative claim.** You don't prove H₁ — you accumulate evidence against H₀ until it becomes untenable.

3. **p-value ≠ probability that H₀ is true.** It is the probability of your data (or more extreme) *given H₀ is true*. Small p-value means the data is surprising under H₀.

4. **Type I error (α) and Type II error (β) are a trade-off.** Lowering α reduces false positives but also reduces power (ability to detect real effects). Increasing n is the only way to improve both simultaneously.

5. **Statistical significance ≠ practical significance.** Always report effect size alongside p-value. A massive sample can make a meaningless difference "significant."

6. **Multiple testing inflates false positives.** Running 20 tests at α = 0.05 gives a 64% chance of at least one spurious significant result. Apply Bonferroni or equivalent corrections.

7. **Confidence intervals and hypothesis tests are two sides of the same coin.** An effect is significant at α = 0.05 if and only if the 95% CI for that effect does not include the null value. Always report both.

## Interview Questions — Hypothesis Testing

These questions are drawn from real data science interviews at Google, Meta, Airbnb, Amazon, and Uber. Each answer models the response structure interviewers expect.

---

### Q1. What is a p-value? Give me a precise definition without jargon.

**Answer:**

A p-value is the probability of observing data as extreme as (or more extreme than) what you actually observed, *assuming the null hypothesis is true*.

Concretely: in the Netflix example, H₀ says the new algorithm doesn't improve watch time (μ ≤ 108 min). We observed x̄ = 112 min. The p-value of 0.0016 means: if the algorithm had truly zero effect, there's only a 0.16% chance we'd see a sample mean of 112 or higher just from random variation.

Because 0.16% is smaller than our 5% threshold, we find this too unlikely — and reject H₀.

What p-value is NOT: it's not the probability H₀ is true, it's not the probability the result is a fluke, and it's not the probability you made an error. These are the most common interview traps and getting them right is a strong signal of statistical maturity.

---

### Q2. What is the difference between Type I and Type II errors? Which is worse?

**Answer:**

A Type I error (false positive) is rejecting H₀ when it's actually true — declaring the new algorithm works when it doesn't. Its probability is α, which you control by setting the significance threshold.

A Type II error (false negative) is failing to reject H₀ when it's actually false — missing a real improvement. Its probability is β, and (1 - β) is the power of the test.

Which is worse depends entirely on the domain and stakes:

In medical trials: a Type I error means approving an ineffective drug (patients take it for no benefit, possibly harm). A Type II error means rejecting a life-saving drug. Here, both are severe — which is why medical trials use α = 0.01 or lower and require high power (≥ 80%).

In tech A/B testing: a Type I error means shipping a feature that doesn't actually help (wasted engineering effort, but usually reversible). A Type II error means shelving a feature that would have genuinely improved the product. For a low-cost feature, the cost of a Type II error might be higher — so companies sometimes use α = 0.10 for early-stage product experiments.

The key point for interviews: there is no universally "worse" error. The right answer articulates the trade-off and ties the choice of α to the business consequence of each error type.

---

### Q3. What is statistical power and what affects it?

**Answer:**

Power is the probability that your test correctly rejects H₀ when H₁ is actually true — the probability of detecting a real effect. Power = 1 - β.

80% power is the conventional minimum: you're willing to miss a real effect 20% of the time. High-stakes applications may require 90% or 95% power.

Four factors that increase power:

1. **Larger sample size** — the primary lever. More data reduces the standard error, making it easier to distinguish a real effect from noise.
2. **Larger true effect size** — a bigger difference is easier to detect. A 10-minute improvement in delivery time is easier to find than a 30-second improvement.
3. **Lower data variability (σ)** — cleaner data, controlled experiments, or measuring more precise proxies all reduce noise and increase power.
4. **Higher α** — relaxing the significance threshold makes it easier to reject H₀, but increases Type I errors. This lever is rarely the right choice.

In practice, data scientists use power analysis to determine sample size before an experiment: "Given a minimum detectable effect of X and desired power of 80%, how many users do we need?"

---

### Q4. What is the difference between a one-tailed and two-tailed test? When do you use each?

**Answer:**

The difference is in what the alternate hypothesis claims, and consequently which part of the t/z distribution the p-value is drawn from.

**One-tailed test:** H₁ specifies a direction — "greater than" or "less than." The p-value is calculated from one tail only. Use when you only care about an effect in one direction: a product change that could only improve (or worsen) a metric, or an SLA that must be below a threshold.

*Example:* Testing whether a new algorithm increases watch time. You only care about increases — a decrease would also be a reason to reject the change. So H₁: μ_new > μ_old.

**Two-tailed test:** H₁ says "different from" — no direction specified. The p-value covers both tails (and is doubled compared to the one-tailed version). Use when a difference in either direction is meaningful.

*Example:* Testing whether mobile and desktop users differ in watch time. A difference in either direction is interesting — you want to detect it regardless.

**The practical caution:** one-tailed tests are more powerful (easier to reject H₀ for the same data), which makes them tempting to misuse. If you run a one-tailed test because "we hoped the new feature would help" — but it could plausibly hurt — you've made a design error. Two-tailed tests are the safer and more common default in industry A/B testing.

---

### Q5. You run an A/B test and get p = 0.04 with α = 0.05. Your manager says "great, ship it." What questions do you ask?

**Answer:**

p = 0.04 < 0.05 means we reject H₀ — but a good data scientist doesn't stop there. The questions I'd raise:

**1. What is the effect size?** p = 0.04 tells us the effect is real; it says nothing about whether it's meaningful. Is the improvement 0.01% or 10%? A tiny effect significant only because of a huge sample isn't worth shipping.

**2. What is the absolute and relative lift?** If conversion went from 12.00% to 12.01%, p might still be < 0.05 with a million users. But that 0.01 pp improvement almost certainly doesn't justify the engineering cost or the UX risk.

**3. Are there subgroup effects?** An average improvement might hide harm to a particular user segment. Check the metrics broken down by device, region, user tenure, etc.

**4. How long did the test run?** Stopping the test early because p dipped below 0.05 is a classic mistake — p-values fluctuate during an experiment. Was the pre-determined sample size reached?

**5. Is there a novelty effect?** Users sometimes respond positively to anything new. Did the test run long enough to distinguish genuine preference from novelty?

**6. Did we correct for multiple tests?** If this was one of fifteen experiments running simultaneously, a Bonferroni-corrected threshold would be α = 0.05/15 = 0.0033 — and p = 0.04 wouldn't be significant at all.

A good data scientist's job is to make the evidence transparent, not just confirm what people want to hear.

---

### Q6. Explain the relationship between a confidence interval and a hypothesis test.

**Answer:**

They are mathematically equivalent — two ways of expressing the same underlying calculation.

A 95% CI and a two-tailed hypothesis test at α = 0.05 will always give consistent answers:
- If the null hypothesis value (μ₀) lies **outside** the 95% CI → the test rejects H₀ at α = 0.05
- If μ₀ lies **inside** the 95% CI → the test fails to reject H₀ at α = 0.05

*Concrete example:* In the Swiggy problem, our 95% CI was [27.73, 29.07] minutes. The null value was μ₀ = 30 minutes. Since 30 is outside the interval, the hypothesis test (also) rejects H₀. Both tools agree — as they always must.

The practical difference is in what they communicate:
- The **CI** is richer: it tells you *where* the true value likely is — useful for estimation and business planning.
- The **hypothesis test** gives a binary verdict: significant or not — useful for go/no-go decisions.

In industry, the best practice is to report both: the test gives the decision, the CI gives the magnitude and uncertainty.

---

### Q7. What is p-hacking and how do you prevent it?

**Answer:**

P-hacking (also called data dredging) is the practice of manipulating an analysis until p < 0.05 is achieved — without a pre-specified plan. It leads to false discoveries that don't replicate.

Common forms:
- Running the test repeatedly and stopping the moment p < 0.05 (peeking)
- Trying multiple statistical tests on the same data and reporting only the one with p < 0.05
- Splitting data into subgroups until one shows significance
- Changing the outcome metric after the results come in
- Adjusting α after seeing the data

Prevention:
1. **Pre-register the experiment** — document H₀, H₁, α, primary metric, and sample size *before* collecting data
2. **Fix the sample size** using a power calculation and don't stop early
3. **Use a single primary metric** — secondary metrics are exploratory only
4. **Apply multiple testing corrections** if analyzing many subgroups
5. **Separate exploratory from confirmatory analysis** — exploratory findings must be validated in a separate experiment before being treated as evidence

At companies like Netflix and Airbnb, experiment platforms enforce pre-registration to protect analysis integrity.

---

### Q8. What happens to the p-value as sample size increases, even if the true effect stays the same?

**Answer:**

The p-value decreases (becomes more significant) as n increases, even if the underlying effect size doesn't change.

Here's why: the test statistic is t = (x̄ - μ₀) / (s/√n). As n grows, the denominator (SE = s/√n) shrinks, making t larger — even if x̄ - μ₀ stays exactly the same. A larger t → smaller p-value.

This is why **statistical significance is not the same as practical significance**. With a large enough sample, you will eventually achieve p < 0.05 for any non-zero effect — including ones that are completely meaningless in practice.

*Netflix example:* If we had sampled 10 million users instead of 1,200 and still observed a 4-minute improvement (identical x̄), the p-value would be essentially zero. But Cohen's d would still be 0.085 — a small effect. The p-value shrank; the effect size didn't change.

The lesson: always pair p-values with effect sizes. Report the size of the difference, not just its existence.

---

### Q9. What is Welch's t-test and why should you prefer it over the classic two-sample t-test?

**Answer:**

The classical two-sample t-test (Student's) assumes the two groups have equal population variances. This is rarely verifiable and often violated in practice.

Welch's t-test relaxes this assumption — it adjusts both the test statistic formula and the degrees of freedom (using the Welch-Satterthwaite approximation) to account for potentially unequal variances. When the variances actually are equal, Welch's test loses almost no power. When they're unequal, it's substantially more accurate.

In the YouTube example, mobile users had s₁ = 18 min and desktop had s₂ = 22 min — clearly unequal. The classical test would produce a slightly wrong answer. Welch's handles this correctly.

The practical recommendation: **always use Welch's t-test for two-sample comparisons**. There is no meaningful downside over Student's t-test when variances are equal, and it's materially better when they're not. `scipy.stats.ttest_ind(a, b, equal_var=False)` is Welch's by default when `equal_var=False`.

---

### Q10. If p = 0.06 and your α = 0.05, do you conclude there is no effect?

**Answer:**

No — and this is a critical nuance. Failing to reject H₀ does not mean H₀ is true. It means the evidence is insufficient to reject it at the chosen threshold.

p = 0.06 means: if H₀ were true, there's a 6% chance of seeing this data. That's slightly above our 5% threshold — but it's not strong evidence *for* H₀ either. The data is somewhat surprising under H₀; we just haven't cleared the bar we set.

The right interpretation is: "We do not have sufficient evidence at α = 0.05 to conclude the effect is real. The effect may still exist — we may simply be underpowered." Next steps could include:
- Computing the confidence interval to see where the effect likely sits — it might still be practically meaningful
- Running a power analysis to determine if a larger sample would detect the effect
- Presenting the result as "inconclusive" rather than "no effect"

Declaring "no effect" from a non-significant result is a common and serious error. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.

---

### Q11. A data scientist says "we achieved p = 0.001, so our result is three times more significant than p = 0.003." What's wrong with this statement?

**Answer:**

Everything. p-values cannot be compared by ratio this way.

p = 0.001 and p = 0.003 are both well below α = 0.05 — both reject H₀. But the difference between them is not "three times more significant." Statistical significance is a binary decision (reject or fail to reject), not a continuous scale of how right you are.

Additionally, p-values don't measure effect size. p = 0.001 could come from a meaningless 0.001-unit difference in an experiment with ten million users. p = 0.03 could come from a massive, practically important effect in a small study.

If they want to quantify "how big is the effect," the right tool is **effect size** (Cohen's d for means, relative lift for proportions). If they want to quantify "how confident are we about the range," the right tool is the **confidence interval**. The p-value's only job is the binary significance decision.

---

### Q12. How do you decide between a one-sample and two-sample hypothesis test?

**Answer:**

The question to ask: **are you comparing your sample to a known fixed value, or are you comparing two groups of data that were both observed?**

**One-sample test:** You have a single sample and a known or hypothesised population value to compare it to.
- Swiggy: "Is our mean delivery time below 30 min?" — 30 is the known threshold, not sampled data.
- Netflix: "Did watch time improve from 108 min?" — 108 is the known historical baseline.

**Two-sample test:** You have two groups, both measured from samples.
- YouTube: "Is mobile watch time different from desktop watch time?" — both 42 min and 38 min came from samples.
- A classic A/B test: treatment group vs control group — both measured, both carry uncertainty.

The two-sample test accounts for variability in *both* groups. A one-sample test only needs to account for variability in the one group being tested. Using a one-sample test when you have two measured groups underestimates uncertainty and inflates Type I errors.

---

### Q13. Walk me through how you would design and analyse an A/B test end-to-end.

**Answer:**

**Phase 1 — Design (before any data is collected)**

1. Define the primary metric — what one number decides success? (e.g., conversion rate, watch time per session). Define secondary guardrail metrics that must not be harmed.
2. State H₀ and H₁ explicitly. Decide one-tailed or two-tailed based on whether the change can only help or can go either way.
3. Set α (usually 0.05) and desired power (usually 80%).
4. Estimate the minimum detectable effect (MDE) — the smallest improvement worth caring about.
5. Run a power calculation to determine required sample size per group.
6. Decide test duration — long enough to reach target n and cover at least one full weekly cycle (avoid day-of-week bias).

**Phase 2 — Running the experiment**

7. Randomly assign users to control (A) and treatment (B) groups.
8. Do NOT peek at results to decide when to stop. Stop when the pre-determined n is reached.
9. Monitor for data quality issues (logging errors, unexpected imbalances in assignment).

**Phase 3 — Analysis**

10. Confirm sample sizes are as expected, assignment is balanced, and baseline metrics in both groups are similar (sanity checks).
11. Run the appropriate hypothesis test (proportion z-test for conversion rate, t-test for continuous metrics).
12. Report p-value, effect size, 95% CI, and absolute/relative lift.
13. Check for subgroup effects and multiple testing corrections if needed.
14. Make the recommendation: ship, don't ship, or run a follow-up experiment.

This is the structured answer that demonstrates you've run real experiments, not just know the theory.

---

### Q14. What is the Bonferroni correction and when would you apply it?

**Answer:**

Bonferroni is a simple correction for the multiple testing problem: when you run k hypothesis tests simultaneously, the probability of at least one false positive grows rapidly above α.

The fix: divide your significance threshold by the number of tests.

```
α_per_test = α_overall / k
```

For 10 tests at α = 0.05: use α_per_test = 0.005 for each individual test.

Apply it when:
- Running multiple A/B tests simultaneously
- Testing multiple subgroups (by device, country, user type) after the fact
- Testing the same hypothesis with multiple metrics
- Running multiple variants against a single control (A/B/C/D test)

Bonferroni is conservative — it sometimes misses real effects by being too strict. More powerful alternatives include the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure (controls the False Discovery Rate rather than the Family-Wise Error Rate), which is widely used in genomics and increasingly in tech A/B testing platforms.

When to not apply it: Bonferroni is for confirmatory tests. Exploratory analyses — where you're generating hypotheses, not testing pre-specified ones — don't require correction, but their findings must be validated in subsequent confirmatory experiments.

---

### Q15. You run a test and the p-value is 0.03 but the confidence interval for the effect includes zero. How is that possible, and what do you do?

**Answer:**

In theory, this should not happen: a two-tailed test at α = 0.05 rejecting H₀ (p < 0.05) is mathematically equivalent to a 95% CI that excludes the null value. If they contradict each other, something went wrong.

Common causes:

1. **Mismatched test types:** Running a one-tailed test (p based on one tail) but reporting a two-tailed CI. The p-value of a one-tailed test at α = 0.05 corresponds to a *90%* CI, not a 95% CI. The fix: use matching tail directions — one-tailed test with 90% one-sided CI, or two-tailed test with 95% two-sided CI.

2. **Different formulas:** Using a z-test for the p-value but a t-distribution for the CI, or different standard error formulas. This introduces inconsistency.

3. **Computational error or wrong function:** Checking whether the correct `scipy.stats` functions were used with matching parameters.

What to do: go back to the raw data, recompute both from scratch using the same formula and the same assumptions, and resolve the inconsistency before reporting anything. Never report a p-value and CI that disagree — it signals an error in the analysis, and any reviewer will catch it immediately.
